Security Foundations

Every time you log into a website, send a message, or store a file online, security mechanisms are working silently to protect you. This page explains how they work — and why they matter for everything you will build in this course.

1. Encryption and TLS

Let us consider a scenario: you type your bank's URL into a browser, enter your username and password, and click login. In that moment, your credentials travel across the internet — through Wi-Fi signals, routers, and fiber cables — before reaching the bank's server. Any network you pass through could have someone listening. Without protection, they would read your password in plain text. This type of attack — intercepting a communication between two parties — is called a man-in-the-middle attack.

The solution is encryption: transforming data into a scrambled form that only the intended recipient can decode. Encryption is the foundation of internet security — authentication, certificates, and HTTPS all build on top of it. Understanding how it works makes every other security concept in this course click into place.

What Is Encryption?

Encryption converts readable text (plaintext) into scrambled text (ciphertext) using a key and an algorithm. Only someone with the right key can reverse the process and read the original message. A simple example: if your key is "shift each letter right by 2," then roses are red becomes tqugu ctg tgf. Real-world algorithms like AES and RSA use complex mathematics — the encrypted output cannot practically be reversed without the key.

There are two fundamentally different approaches, and both are used in every HTTPS connection you make:

Symmetric Encryption

HowBoth sender and receiver use the same key to encrypt and decrypt
ProVery fast — simple mathematical operations, practical for large data
ProblemThe chicken-and-egg problem: how do you share the key securely before you have a secure channel?
Used forBulk data encryption once a session is established

Asymmetric Encryption

HowTwo mathematically linked keys — the public key encrypts, only the private key decrypts
ProNo shared secret needed — anyone can encrypt with the public key, only the owner can decrypt
ProblemSlow — complex mathematical operations, not practical for encrypting large amounts of data
Used forKey exchange and digital signatures — then hand off to symmetric encryption
The key insight: The internet solves the chicken-and-egg problem by using asymmetric encryption just long enough to securely share a symmetric key — then switching to the faster symmetric encryption for the rest of the session. This is exactly what TLS does.

How TLS Works — The Handshake

TLS (Transport Layer Security) is the protocol that makes HTTPS work. When you see the padlock in your browser, TLS is running underneath. Here is what happens step by step when you connect to a bank:

1
TCP handshake — connection established

Your browser and the server complete a TCP three-way handshake (SYN → SYN-ACK → ACK). A connection exists but nothing is encrypted yet — this is plain HTTP.

2
Server presents its digital certificate

The bank's server sends a digital certificate issued by a trusted Certificate Authority (CA) — organizations like Google or VeriSign. The certificate contains the bank's public key, domain name, and validity period.

3
Browser validates the certificate

Your browser has the public keys of major CAs pre-installed. It uses the CA's public key to verify the certificate's signature. If valid, it extracts the bank's public key. If invalid, you see a browser security warning — do not proceed past it.

4
Key exchange — session key shared securely

The browser generates a random session key, encrypts it with the bank's public key, and sends it over. Only the bank — holding the corresponding private key — can decrypt this and recover the session key. Now both sides share a symmetric key without ever sending it in the clear.

5
Encrypted session begins — HTTPS

All further communication — credentials, account data, every request and response — is encrypted with the shared session key. The padlock appears. This is HTTPS: HTTP with TLS underneath.

Why Certificates Matter — Stopping Impersonation

Encryption alone does not guarantee you are talking to the right server. A fake bank site could also encrypt its traffic. Two attacks exploit this gap:

Phishing: A convincing email appears to come from your bank. You click the link, land on a site that looks identical to your bank, enter your credentials — and hand them directly to attackers.

DNS spoofing: When your browser resolves chase.com to an IP address, an attacker who compromises that DNS lookup can return the IP of a fake server instead. You end up at the right URL but the wrong site.

Certificates solve both. A fake site cannot obtain a certificate for chase.com from a CA — the CA verifies that the requester actually controls that domain. Think of it like university transcripts: you cannot self-certify your own grades, your institution must send them. Your browser refuses to show the padlock for any site whose certificate does not check out with a trusted CA.

TLS in one sentence

TLS uses asymmetric encryption to verify identity and securely share a session key, then switches to symmetric encryption for speed — giving you a connection that is both private and authenticated.

2. The CIA Triad

The CIA triad is the foundational framework of information security. Every security control — every firewall rule, encryption policy, access restriction, and backup procedure — exists to protect one or more of these three properties. Notice that we have already seen all three at work in the TLS example above.

Click each property to see what it means, how it is threatened, and how it is protected.

🔒

Confidentiality

Only authorized parties can access the data

✅

Integrity

Data is accurate and has not been tampered with

⚡

Availability

Systems are accessible when legitimate users need them

Confidentiality means keeping sensitive information away from those who should not have it — whether data is moving across a network or sitting in a database.

  • Encryption in transit: TLS ensures credentials and session data cannot be read by anyone intercepting network traffic
  • Encryption at rest: Financial records in a database should be encrypted so that even if an attacker gains physical access to the storage, the data is unreadable
  • Authentication: Verify identity before granting access. Username and password alone is weak — MFA adds a second factor that a stolen password cannot bypass
  • Authorization: Even authenticated users should only see their own data. A bank customer should not see another customer's balance. Being logged in does not mean unlimited access

Integrity means you can trust that the data you receive is exactly what was sent — not modified in transit or storage, and genuinely from who it claims to be from.

  • Digital signatures: A bank signs a transfer request with its private key. The receiving bank verifies with the public key — only the true sender could have produced that signature, proving both origin and that the amount was not altered
  • Digital certificates: Certificates verify you are talking to the legitimate website, not an impersonator. Issued and signed by a Certificate Authority whose public key is embedded in your browser
  • Password hashing: Banks store hashed passwords, not plain text. Hashing is a one-way operation — even if the database is stolen, attackers cannot reverse the hashes to get original passwords
  • Audit trails and logging: Every access to sensitive systems is logged — who, what, when. Immutable logs support detection, investigation, and accountability

Availability means legitimate users can access services when they need them. A system that is down — for any reason — has failed its security obligation as surely as one that was breached.

  • DDoS attacks: Attackers flood a server with millions of requests. The server cannot distinguish these from real traffic and gets overwhelmed. SYN floods exploit TCP handshakes — send SYN, never complete the ACK, leaving the server waiting across thousands of half-open connections
  • DDoS mitigation: Rate limiting, traffic scrubbing, and CDNs absorb and distribute attack traffic before it overwhelms the origin server
  • Redundancy: Multiple servers across multiple locations ensure a single failure does not take down the service — traffic routes automatically to healthy instances
  • Ransomware: Attackers gain access, encrypt all of an organization's data, and demand payment for the decryption key. The organization cannot operate until data is restored
  • Backups: Frequent, tested backups allow restoration after ransomware or hardware failure — the organization can recover without paying the attacker
The triad in the TLS example: TLS delivers all three — Confidentiality via encryption so credentials cannot be read in transit, Integrity via certificates so you know you are talking to the real bank and the message has not been altered, and Availability is maintained by the reliable, responsive connection. Most security incidents violate at least one of these three properties.

3. The AAA Framework

CIA defines what you are protecting. AAA is the operational mechanism that enforces one of those properties — Confidentiality. You cannot control who accesses data without first knowing who someone is (Authentication), then deciding what they are allowed to do (Authorization), and recording what they actually did (Accounting). Accounting also touches Integrity — the audit trail is evidence that data was accessed legitimately and has not been tampered with.

🪪

Authentication

Who are you? Prove it.

🚦

Authorization

What are you allowed to do?

📋

Accounting

What did you do, and when?

Authentication is the process of verifying that someone is who they claim to be. A username alone is just a claim — authentication requires proof.

Weak authentication relies only on a password. Passwords can be guessed, phished, or stolen in a database breach. Multi-factor authentication (MFA) adds a second factor — something you have (a phone or hardware key) in addition to something you know (a password). Even if a password is stolen, an attacker cannot log in without the second factor.

After identity is verified, authorization determines what that identity is permitted to do. Being authenticated does not mean being allowed to do everything.

A bank customer who successfully logs in can see their own account — but not another customer's. They cannot access the password database or administrative systems. An employee in a read-only role can view records but not delete them. This control based on identity is called Role-Based Access Control (RBAC).

The principle of least privilege states that every identity should have only the minimum permissions needed for its job — nothing more. Over-permissioned accounts are one of the most common sources of cloud security incidents.

Accounting means recording what authenticated and authorized users actually did. It creates the audit trail necessary to detect anomalies, investigate incidents, and demonstrate compliance.

Who logged in? From where? What data did they access? What did they change or delete? A complete, tamper-proof audit trail answers these questions after the fact. An attacker who can erase logs removes all evidence of what they did — which is why log immutability is a key security requirement.

4. Security Controls

We now know what we are protecting (CIA) and how identity is managed (AAA). But security is not just about identity — it requires a whole range of mechanisms working together. These mechanisms are called security controls: any safeguard or countermeasure designed to protect the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of information. Controls are organized in two dimensions: type (what kind of control it is) and function (what it is designed to do).

Control Types — How They Work

🏢 Physical Controls Tangible, real-world barriers ▶

Physical controls are tangible mechanisms that prevent unauthorized physical access to systems and infrastructure — locks, guards, cameras, and barriers.

Examples: Data center locks, badge access readers, security guards, CCTV, biometric entry, server cage locks, mantrap entries.

In cloud context: AWS is entirely responsible for physical controls. You never interact with the hardware — this is one of the core security benefits of cloud computing. Physical security is fully on AWS's side of the Shared Responsibility Model.

💻 Technical Controls Software and hardware mechanisms ▶

Technical controls are automated safeguards enforced by systems — they operate without requiring human action each time and are the primary category of security work in cloud environments.

Examples: Firewalls, encryption, MFA, access control policies, VPNs, intrusion detection systems, antivirus, security groups.

In cloud context: The majority of what you configure in AWS are technical controls — IAM policies, security groups, encryption settings, CloudTrail — and they are your primary responsibility under the Shared Responsibility Model.

📄 Administrative Controls Policies, procedures, and practices ▶

Administrative controls govern how people behave and how processes are run — they are the organizational foundation that makes technical controls effective.

Examples: Security policies, employee training, incident response plans, access review procedures, change management processes, acceptable use policies.

In cloud context: Your organization's policies for AWS account creation, credential rotation, and incident escalation are administrative controls. AWS provides the tools; you write the policies that govern how they are used.

Control Functions — What They Do

🛡 Preventive Stop incidents before they happen ▶

Preventive controls reduce the likelihood of a security incident occurring in the first place. They are the first line of defense.

Examples: MFA, least-privilege access policies, encryption, firewall rules blocking unauthorized traffic, bucket policies denying public access, disabling unused credentials.

🔍 Detective Identify incidents as they occur ▶

Detective controls identify that a security incident has occurred or is currently occurring, enabling a timely response. They cannot prevent the incident but limit the damage.

Examples: Audit logging, threat detection services, anomaly alarms, compliance monitoring rules, security dashboards aggregating findings across services.

🔧 Corrective Restore systems after an incident ▶

Corrective controls restore systems and operations to normal after a security incident has been detected. They minimize the impact and duration of a breach.

Examples: Restoring from backup after ransomware, revoking compromised credentials, patching a vulnerable system, isolating a compromised instance, rolling back a misconfigured change.

5. Layered Security — Defense in Depth

You have now seen three types of controls and three functions they serve. The natural question is: which ones do you actually need? The answer is all of them — and the reason is the principle of defense in depth. No single control is sufficient on its own. Security should be applied at every layer of a system so that if one control fails, others still hold. An attacker who gets past the network firewall should still face application-level access controls. An attacker who steals a password should still be stopped by MFA. An attacker who encrypts your data should still be stopped by your backups.

🌐 Outermost layer

Network Security

Controls which traffic is allowed to reach your systems at all. Firewalls, VPCs, subnets, and NACLs sit at the perimeter — filtering traffic before it gets anywhere near your applications or data. An attacker who cannot reach your network cannot attack it.

💻 Layer 2

Device Security

Protects the machines themselves — servers, laptops, and endpoints. Even if network controls allow traffic through, a vulnerable or unpatched device is an open door. OS patching, endpoint protection (antivirus, EDR), and disabling unused services keep devices hardened.

⚙️ Layer 3

Application Security

Secures the software running on those devices. Applications are where most modern attacks occur — SQL injection, cross-site scripting, logic flaws. WAF rules, secure coding practices, input validation, and vulnerability scanning address this layer.

🗄 Layer 4

Data Security

Protects the data itself — at rest and in transit. Even if an attacker gets through every outer layer, encrypted data is useless without the key. Encryption, strict access policies, data classification, and backups are the last line before the most sensitive information.

🪪 Innermost — the core

Identity Security

Controls who can access what — and underpins every other layer. Authentication (MFA), authorization (least privilege), and SSO determine whether someone should be allowed in at all. If identity is compromised — a stolen credential, an over-permissioned role — an attacker can walk past every outer layer as a legitimate user.

6. Compliance Frameworks

Knowing how to build a secure, layered system is necessary — but in many industries it is not sufficient on its own. Regulators, customers, and partners often require you to demonstrate that your controls meet a defined external standard. This is what compliance frameworks are: structured sets of requirements that an independent party can audit and verify.

Compliance is not the same as security. Security is about genuinely reducing risk. Compliance is about demonstrating to a third party that you meet a defined standard. A system can be fully compliant but still insecure — and a highly secure system may not satisfy a compliance auditor if it lacks required documentation. Compliance frameworks provide a useful baseline of controls, but the goal is always genuine security, not a checkbox.

The four frameworks below are the most commonly encountered in industry. Each applies to a specific type of data or sector — but all of them map back to the CIA triad. Click each to expand.

HIPAA Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act Healthcare ▶

Who it applies to: Any organization handling Protected Health Information (PHI) — medical records, health data, insurance claims. Healthcare providers, insurers, and their business associates including cloud vendors storing PHI.

Key requirements: Access controls on PHI, audit logging of all access, encryption of PHI at rest and in transit, breach notification within 72 hours, Business Associate Agreements (BAAs) with vendors.

CIA connection: Primarily enforces Confidentiality (who can access PHI) and Integrity (audit trails, breach notification). Availability requirements exist but are secondary.

PCI DSS Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard Payments ▶

Who it applies to: Any organization that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data — payment processors, e-commerce sites, retailers, banks.

Key requirements: Network segmentation to isolate cardholder data, no storage of full card numbers after authorization, encryption in transit (TLS 1.2+), quarterly vulnerability scans, annual penetration testing, MFA for all administrative access.

CIA connection: Enforces all three — Confidentiality (card number protection), Integrity (tamper detection, penetration testing), Availability (network resilience requirements).

ISO 27001 Information Security Management System Enterprise / Government ▶

Who it applies to: Any organization seeking to demonstrate a systematic approach to information security management. Commonly required in enterprise procurement and government contracts globally.

Key requirements: Formal risk assessment and treatment, documented security policies, access control policy, incident response plan, business continuity planning, regular internal audits and management reviews.

CIA connection: Uniquely holistic — addresses all three CIA properties through a management system framework, not just specific technical controls.

GDPR General Data Protection Regulation EU personal data ▶

Who it applies to: Any organization processing personal data of EU residents — regardless of where the organization is based. A US company with EU customers must comply.

Key requirements: Lawful basis for processing personal data, right to access, erasure, and portability, data minimization, privacy by design, breach notification within 72 hours, Data Protection Impact Assessments for high-risk processing.

CIA connection: Primarily enforces Confidentiality (who can access personal data and why) and Integrity (accuracy, breach notification). The right to erasure creates a unique tension with backup and availability controls.

AWS compliance posture: AWS holds certifications for many of these frameworks — HIPAA eligibility, PCI DSS Level 1, ISO 27001, and more. These certifications apply to AWS's infrastructure. You inherit that posture for the infrastructure layer, but you must implement the controls for your application layer yourself. AWS compliance does not make your application compliant.
